You are an expert in Answer Set Programming (ASP).

Given a curated mate-in-one base fragment (board state plus helper predicates that derive legal moves), write a complete Clingo program that determines whether the side to move has a mate in one. Copy the base fragment verbatim at the top of your output, then extend it with the reasoning layer.

A mate in one occurs if a legal move by the side to move leaves the opponent king in check and the opponent has no legal reply that removes the check.

Below are representative ASP examples that illustrate the structures you should use.

Example 1: Selecting exactly one move using a choice rule over legal_move/5.

% Pick exactly one legal white move.
1{ move(ID, R1, C1, R2, C2):
        legal_move(ID, R1, C1, R2, C2),
        placement(white, _, ID, R1, C1)
}1.

---

Example 2: Deriving the board state after the chosen move (new_placement)
and detecting captured pieces.

% The moved piece is now at its destination.
new_placement(ID, R2, C2) :- move(ID, _, _, R2, C2).

% Every other piece stays, unless it was captured.
new_placement(ID, R, C) :-
    placement(_, _, ID, R, C),
    not move(ID, _, _, _, _),
    not captured(_, ID, _, _).

% A piece is captured when another piece moves onto its square.
captured(ID1, ID2, R, C) :-
    new_placement(ID1, R, C),
    placement(_, _, ID2, R, C), ID1 != ID2.

---

Example 3: Computing threats after the move (using the "after" state helpers
already in the base fragment) and detecting check.

% White piece threatens a square after the move.
threaten(ID, R1, C1, R2, C2) :-
    new_placement(ID, R1, C1),
    legal_queen_move(white, after, R1, C1, R2, C2),
    placement(white, queen, ID, _, _).
% ... analogous rules for rook, bishop, knight, pawn, king.

% Black king is in check if any white piece threatens its square.
in_check(black) :- threaten(_, _, _, R, C), placement(black, king, bk, R, C).

---

Example 4: Checking whether the opponent can escape (king flight, blocking,
capturing the attacker) and defining checkmate.

% King flight: black king can move to a square not attacked by white.
can_escape(black) :-
    new_placement(bk, R1, C1),
    legal_king_move(black, R1, C1, R2, C2),
    not white_threatens(R2, C2).

% Block: a black piece (not the king) moves onto the attack path.
can_escape(black) :-
    placement(black, rook, ID, R1, C1), ID != bk,
    legal_rook_move(black, after, R1, C1, R2, C2),
    threaten(A_ID, A_R1, A_C1, A_R2, A_C2),
    placement(black, king, bk, A_R2, A_C2),
    on_attack_path(R2, C2, A_R1, A_C1, A_R2, A_C2).
% ... analogous block rules for queen, bishop, knight, pawn.

% Capture: a black piece (not the king) captures the attacker.
can_escape(black) :-
    placement(black, rook, ID, R1, C1), ID != bk,
    legal_rook_move(black, after, R1, C1, R2, C2),
    placement(black, king, bk, BK_R, BK_C),
    threaten(A_ID, _, _, BK_R, BK_C),
    new_placement(A_ID, R2, C2).
% ... analogous capture rules for queen, bishop, knight, pawn.

% Checkmate: in check with no escape.
checkmate(black) :- in_check(black), not can_escape(black).

% Only keep answer sets where checkmate holds.
:- not checkmate(black).

---

Input predicates already exist inside the base fragment — do NOT redefine them:
to_move(Color).
placement(Color,Piece,Id,Row,Col).
legal_move(Id,R1,C1,R2,C2).

Board coordinates range from 1..8.

Your program must:
	•	generate legal moves and capture the resulting positions,
	•	represent attacks between pieces,
	•	detect check on the opponent king, and
	•	ensure the opponent has no legal move that escapes check (king moves, interpositions, captures).

Output predicates (must match exactly):
  move(Id, R1, C1, R2, C2)   — the chosen mating move (Id is the piece identifier from placement/5, e.g. wq, wr1, wn2).
  in_check(Color)             — the opponent is in check after the move.
  can_escape(Color)           — the opponent can escape check (should be false for mate).
  checkmate(Color)            — the opponent is checkmated.

Only output the ASP program.
